The Big Idea

The Most Connected Numbers Aren't the Most Important

Imagine a road network where every city is connected to its neighbors, but some cities also have express highways that let you jump over many cities at once. You'd probably guess that the city sitting at the intersection of the most highways would be the most important, right?

This paper proves that intuition is wrong.

We build a network (a "graph") on the integers from 1 to N. Each number is connected to its neighbors (1→2→3→…), and also to its multiples via "prime highways." The number 2310 = 2 × 3 × 5 × 7 × 11 sits on five different highways and has 12 connections — the most of any number. But it's not the most important bottleneck.

Instead, simple numbers like 2 × 619 = 1238 — sitting on just two highways with only 6 connections — turn out to be the true bottlenecks. They control more traffic than any high-degree number.

Think of a small-town gas station on a remote desert highway. There might be only one road passing through, but every car on that road must stop there. Meanwhile, a massive interchange in a city has dozens of roads — but each individual road has many alternative routes. The gas station is the bottleneck, not the interchange.

The Results

What We Proved — Step by Step

Click any result to expand it. Each one has a plain-English explanation and the formal mathematical statement.

Definition The Arithmetic Graph ▼
In plain English: Take the numbers 1, 2, 3, … up to N. Connect each number to the next one (like a chain). Then, for every prime number p, also connect every multiple of p to the next multiple — so 2p connects to 3p, 3p connects to 4p, and so on. These "prime shortcuts" are the highways.
G_N has vertex set {1, …, N} with edges from sequential adjacency (n → n+1) and prime-stride multiplication (kp → (k+1)p for each prime p).
Proposition How Many Connections? (Edge Count) ▼
In plain English: The total number of connections in the graph grows slowly — roughly as N × log(log(N)). That means the graph is very sparse: most possible connections don't exist. Yet the few that do exist are enough to make the graph a "small world" where any node can reach any other in a small number of steps.
|E(G_N)| = (N − 1) + Σ(⌊N/p⌋ − 1) over primes p ≤ N. Average degree ~ 2 log log N.

Proposition Degree Formula — How Connected Is Each Number? ▼
In plain English: A number's connection count depends on how many different prime factors it has. If a number has ω prime factors (and isn't near the boundary), its degree is exactly 2 + 2ω. A prime has 1 prime factor → 3 connections. A semiprime (two prime factors) → 6 connections. A number like 2310 with 5 prime factors → 12 connections.
For interior nodes: deg(v) = 2 + 2ω(v), where ω(v) is the number of distinct prime divisors of v.

Proposition Small-World Property — Short Paths Everywhere ▼
In plain English: Despite the graph being sparse, you can get from any number to any other in surprisingly few steps. We proved the diameter is at most O(√N), and the data suggests it might even be O(log N) — meaning in a graph with 10,000 nodes, the longest shortest path is only about 27 steps. The prime highways act as express routes that shrink distances dramatically.

Corollary Large-Prime Necessity — You Must Use the Big Highways ▼
In plain English: If you need to travel a long distance in the graph, you have to use at least one large-prime highway. Small primes (2, 3, 5…) give you dense highways with lots of stops — but each step only moves you a little. To cover real distance, you need the big jumps that only come from large primes. And those large-prime highways have very few stops — which is exactly why they create bottlenecks.
Every shortest path covering distance ≥ N/C must use a multiplicative edge with p > √(N/C).
This is the fundamental link: efficient routing requires large-prime highways, and large-prime highways are inherently sparse. Sparsity creates bottlenecks.
Lemma Highway Sparsity — Big Primes, Few Stops ▼
In plain English: The highway for prime p has exactly ⌊N/p⌋ stops. So the 2-highway (multiples of 2) has ~N/2 stops — half the numbers. But the highway for a large prime like p = 503 (when N = 2000) has only 3 stops: 503, 1006, and 1509. For primes larger than N/3, the highway shrinks to just 2 stops. For primes larger than N/2, only 1 stop — and no edges at all.
For N/3 < p ≤ N/2, the p-highway has exactly 2 stops {p, 2p} and a single edge. Every path using this edge passes through both nodes.
The 2-highway is like a major interstate with an exit every mile. The highway for a large prime is like a desert road with one gas station in 500 miles. Both are useful — but the desert gas station is irreplaceable.
Lemma Access Cost — How Easy Is It to Reach a Highway? ▼
In plain English: To get onto the highway for number k, you walk along the chain until you hit a multiple of k. The farthest you'd ever need to walk is ⌊k/2⌋ steps. For the 2-highway, that's at most 1 step — because every other number is even. For the 3-highway, at most 1 step too. This means the 2-highway is the easiest to access from anywhere.
The distance from any node to the nearest multiple of k is at most ⌊k/2⌋. Access cost to the 2-highway ≤ 1. Access cost to the 3-highway ≤ 1.
Theorem The Semiprime Bottleneck — Why 2p Wins ▼
In plain English: Take a large prime p (between N/4 and N/3). Its highway has exactly 3 stops: p, 2p, and 3p. Among these three, 2p is the winner because it combines two advantages:

1. Best position: 2p sits near the middle of {1,…,N}, making it geometrically closest to a random starting point.

2. Easiest access: 2p is on the 2-highway, so any number can reach it in just 1 step + a ride on the 2-highway. The bare prime p has no such shortcut, and 3p is farther from center.

This is confirmed by the data: 2p captures about 44% of all traffic on its highway, consistently beating both p and 3p.
Among {p, 2p, 3p} on a 3-stop highway, node 2p has the lowest expected shortest-path distance from a uniformly random source, combining lowest access cost with best geometric centrality.
This is the paper's central result. The "sparsity–accessibility trade-off": large primes create sparse highways (few stops = bottleneck), and 2p is the most accessible stop on those sparse highways. Sparsity × accessibility = maximum importance.
Proposition Primorial Dilution — Why High-Degree Nodes Lose ▼
In plain English: A primorial like 2310 sits on 5 different highways — but each of those highways has hundreds of other stops. On the 2-highway alone, 2310 is just one of ~N/2 stops. It's a small fish in a big pond, on every pond it swims in.

In contrast, 2 × 619 = 1238 sits on the 619-highway, which has only 3 stops total. It's a big fish in a tiny pond. The math shows that the primorial's betweenness contribution from each highway vanishes as O(p/N), while the semiprime's contribution stays constant.
For a fixed primorial v = p₁⋯pᵣ with small prime factors, its share of betweenness on the pᵢ-highway is at most O(pᵢ/N), which → 0 as N grows.
Corollary Degree–Betweenness Decorrelation ▼
In plain English: Putting it all together: as the graph gets larger, the correlation between how many connections a node has (degree) and how important it is as a bottleneck (betweenness) drops toward zero. High-degree nodes have vanishing importance, while low-degree semiprimes dominate. By N = 10,000, the correlation has fallen to just 0.022 — almost zero.
The Pearson correlation ρ(deg, BC) decays to 0 as N → ∞.

Conjecture The Scaling Conjecture — Semiprimes Win Forever ▼
In plain English: We conjecture that as the graph grows to infinity, the correlation between degree and betweenness goes to exactly zero, and the bottleneck nodes consist entirely of semiprimes 2p and 3p. The data decay roughly as N−0.8, suggesting this isn't a fluke — it's a fundamental structural law.
As N → ∞, ρ(deg, BC) → 0. The set of nodes maximizing betweenness eventually consists entirely of semiprimes 2p and 3p.

Why It Matters

Discussion & Broader Implications

The Structural Paradox

The nodes that look most important (high degree, many prime factors) are not the ones that function as the most critical bottlenecks. The true bottlenecks are semiprimes — nodes sitting at the intersection of a dense subnetwork (the 2- or 3-highway) and a sparse one (a large-prime highway).

A node's importance depends not on how many highways it serves, but on how irreplaceable it is on the highways it serves. A primorial serves many highways, but each has many other stops. A semiprime 2p serves only two highways, but on the large-prime highway it is one of very few stops — and the most accessible one.

Beyond Arithmetic Graphs

Network Resilience

Standard strategies for protecting networks focus on high-degree "hubs." This research shows that the true vulnerabilities may be low-degree bridge nodes that are hard to identify without computing betweenness. In infrastructure, transportation, and communication networks, the critical failure points might not be the obvious big intersections — they might be small connectors between dense and sparse parts of the network.

Computational Number Theory

The fact that semiprimes 2p occupy a structurally privileged position in the multiplicative landscape of the integers may connect to deeper questions in number theory. The routing geometry of the arithmetic graph encodes how addition and multiplication interact. Understanding which integers act as bottlenecks in this interaction could potentially inform algorithmic approaches to factorization and primality testing.

A Universal Principle

The "sparsity–accessibility trade-off" is not specific to numbers. Any network containing both dense and sparse subnetworks may show the same paradox: low-degree nodes bridging a dense cluster to a sparse one can dominate betweenness over high-degree hubs. Arithmetic graphs provide a clean, deterministic testbed to study this phenomenon analytically — without the noise of random network models.
